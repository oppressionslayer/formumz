The puzzle

It's called "hidden parity".

Imagine 12 light switches. Five of them — secretly chosen — together decide whether a lamp lights up. The rule is: the lamp is on if an odd number of those five switches are flipped. None of the other switches matter.

You're shown thousands of switch settings and whether the lamp was on. Can you figure out which five switches matter, and the rule?

Standard AI can't. Looking at any one switch tells you nothing — each switch alone is 50/50. The pattern only exists in the combination. There's a mathematical theorem (Kearns, 1998) proving this is unsolvable for the entire family of methods that all modern AI is built on.

William's math sees it in under a minute on a 2024 laptop.

Part two · 40 years of mathematicians said impossible

This pattern is called Rule 30.

In 1985, a famous scientist named Stephen Wolfram (he founded Wolfram Research — Mathematica, Wolfram Alpha) studied a simple rule that produces this triangle of black-and-white squares:

The hidden structure

And the "random" pattern? It isn't random.

Apply one simple transformation to each row of the cascade — flip the bits in mirror image, strip the empty space, read the result as a number. That gives this sequence:

Row 1 → 7 Row 2 → 25 Row 3 → 111 Row 4 → 401 Row 5 → 1,783 Row 6 → 6,409 Row 7 → 28,479 Row 8 → 102,849 Row 9 → 456,263 Row 10 → 1,641,433 Row 11 → 7,287,855 Row 12 → 26,332,369

These numbers follow an exact rule: each one is two bits longer than the last, and the pattern of its last two bits alternates perfectly between three and seven, forever.

What Wolfram's framework called "cryptographic randomness" turns out to be a clean, deterministic sequence. The math was always there. People just hadn't looked at it the right way.

Don't replace XGBoost with WSRF — combine them. Average their predictions. WSRF picks up structure XGBoost misses; XGBoost picks up smooth signal. The blend usually beats both.

Pure image / text / audio (use CNNs or transformers). Small datasets under 1,000 rows. Smooth functions where every feature is individually predictive — XGBoost wins those easily.
